Module 1

Descriptive Statistics & Correlation

Descriptive statistics summarise data with measures of central tendency (mean, median, mode) and dispersion (range, variance, SD). Correlation and regression measure the strength and direction of linear relationships between two variables.

1.1 Measures of Central Tendency & Dispersion

DESCRIPTIVE STATISTICS FORMULAS
Arithmetic Mean
\(\bar{x}=\dfrac{\sum x_i}{n}=\dfrac{\sum f_ix_i}{\sum f_i}\)
Variance (population)
\(\sigma^2=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n}=\dfrac{\sum x_i^2}{n}-\bar{x}^2\)
Variance (sample)
\(s^2=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1}\)
Coefficient of Variation
\(\text{CV}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\bar{x}}\times100\%\)
Skewness (Karl Pearson)
\(S_k=\dfrac{\bar{x}-\text{Mode}}{\sigma}\)
Median (grouped)
\(M=L+\dfrac{N/2-F}{f}\times h\)
1
Variance and SD
Find mean, variance, and SD of: 2, 4, 4, 6, 8, 8, 8, 10.
SOLUTION
1
\(\bar{x}=\frac{2+4+4+6+8+8+8+10}{8}=\frac{50}{8}=6.25\)
2
\(\sum x_i^2=4+16+16+36+64+64+64+100=364\)
3
\(\sigma^2=364/8-6.25^2=45.5-39.0625=6.4375\). \(\sigma\approx2.54\)
Mean = 6.25, Variance ≈ 6.44, SD ≈ 2.54

1.2 Correlation & Regression

CORRELATION & REGRESSION FORMULAS
Pearson's r
\(r=\dfrac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{n\sigma_x\sigma_y}=\dfrac{n\sum xy-\sum x\sum y}{\sqrt{[n\sum x^2-(\sum x)^2][n\sum y^2-(\sum y)^2]}}\)
Regression y on x
\(y-\bar{y}=b_{yx}(x-\bar{x})\) where \(b_{yx}=r\dfrac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x}\)
Regression x on y
\(x-\bar{x}=b_{xy}(y-\bar{y})\) where \(b_{xy}=r\dfrac{\sigma_x}{\sigma_y}\)
r from regression coeff.
\(r^2=b_{yx}\cdot b_{xy}\) (both same sign as r)
Spearman's rank corr.
\(r_s=1-\dfrac{6\sum d_i^2}{n(n^2-1)}\)
|r| interpretation
0: no corr; 0.5–0.7: moderate; 0.7–0.9: strong; >0.9: very strong
1
Pearson's correlation coefficient
For n=5, Σx=15, Σy=25, Σx²=55, Σy²=135, Σxy=83. Find r.
SOLUTION
1
Numerator: \(n\Sigma xy-\Sigma x\Sigma y=5(83)-15(25)=415-375=40\)
2
\(D_x=n\Sigma x^2-(\Sigma x)^2=5(55)-225=275-225=50\)
3
\(D_y=n\Sigma y^2-(\Sigma y)^2=5(135)-625=675-625=50\)
4
\(r=40/\sqrt{50\times50}=40/50=0.8\)
r = 0.8 (strong positive correlation)
2
Spearman's rank correlation
Ranks: d values = 1, −2, 0, 1, 2 for n=5. Find rₛ.
SOLUTION
1
\(\sum d_i^2=1+4+0+1+4=10\)
2
\(r_s=1-\frac{6(10)}{5(24)}=1-\frac{60}{120}=0.5\)
rₛ = 0.5 (moderate positive correlation)

Module 2

Probability & Random Variables

Probability theory provides the mathematical language for uncertainty. Random variables formalise outcomes numerically; expectation and variance capture the average behaviour and spread, forming the backbone of statistical inference.

2.1 Probability Axioms & Bayes' Theorem

PROBABILITY LAWS
Bayes' Theorem
\(P(A|B)=\dfrac{P(B|A)P(A)}{P(B)}\)
Total Probability
\(P(B)=\displaystyle\sum_i P(B|A_i)P(A_i)\)
Independence
\(P(A\cap B)=P(A)\cdot P(B)\)
1
Bayes' Theorem
A test is 95% accurate for disease (D). Disease prevalence = 1%. P(D|positive)?
SOLUTION
1
P(D)=0.01, P(+|D)=0.95, P(+|D̄)=0.05 (false positive)
2
P(+)=0.95(0.01)+0.05(0.99)=0.0095+0.0495=0.059
3
P(D|+)=0.0095/0.059≈0.161
P(D|+) ≈ 16.1% (surprisingly low due to low prevalence)

2.2 Random Variables, PDF, CDF, E(X), Var(X)

RANDOM VARIABLE FORMULAS
CDF (continuous)
\(F(x)=P(X\le x)=\int_{-\infty}^x f(t)\,dt\)
Expectation E(X)
\(E(X)=\int_{-\infty}^\infty xf(x)\,dx\) (continuous)
Variance Var(X)
\(\text{Var}(X)=E(X^2)-[E(X)]^2\)
E(aX+b)
\(aE(X)+b\)
Var(aX+b)
\(a^2\text{Var}(X)\)
Moment Generating Function
\(M_X(t)=E(e^{tX})\)
1
Find E(X) and Var(X)
Let \(f(x)=2x\) for \(0\le x\le1\). Find E(X) and Var(X).
SOLUTION
1
\(E(X)=\int_0^1 x\cdot2x\,dx=2\int_0^1 x^2\,dx=2/3\)
2
\(E(X^2)=\int_0^1 x^2\cdot2x\,dx=2\int_0^1 x^3\,dx=1/2\)
3
\(\text{Var}(X)=1/2-(2/3)^2=1/2-4/9=1/18\)
E(X) = 2/3 ≈ 0.667; Var(X) = 1/18 ≈ 0.056

Module 3

Probability Distributions

Standard probability distributions model real-world phenomena: Binomial for coin flips and defects, Poisson for rare events and arrival rates, Normal for measurement errors and heights, Exponential for lifetimes and waiting times.

3.1 Discrete Distributions

DISCRETE DISTRIBUTIONS
Binomial B(n,p)
\(P(X=k)=\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\)
E(X)=np; Var=npq
Poisson P(λ)
\(P(X=k)=\dfrac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}\)
E(X)=λ; Var(X)=λ
Binomial→Poisson
When n large, p small, np=λ constant
1
Binomial Distribution
If a die is thrown 5 times, find P(exactly 2 sixes).
SOLUTION
1
n=5, k=2, p=1/6, q=5/6
2
\(P(X=2)=\binom{5}{2}(1/6)^2(5/6)^3=10\cdot\frac{1}{36}\cdot\frac{125}{216}=\frac{1250}{7776}\approx0.1608\)
P(X=2) ≈ 0.1608 ≈ 16.08%
2
Poisson Distribution
Calls arrive at 3 per minute. Find P(exactly 5 calls in 1 minute).
SOLUTION
1
λ=3. \(P(X=5)=\frac{e^{-3}\cdot3^5}{5!}=\frac{0.0498\cdot243}{120}=\frac{12.1}{120}\approx0.1008\)
P(X=5) ≈ 0.1008 ≈ 10.08%

3.2 Continuous Distributions

CONTINUOUS DISTRIBUTIONS
Normal N(μ,σ²)
\(f(x)=\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}\); Standard: \(Z=\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\)
Exponential (λ)
\(f(x)=\lambda e^{-\lambda x},\;x\ge0\); E(X)=1/λ; Var=1/λ²
Gamma Γ(α,β)
\(f(x)=\frac{x^{\alpha-1}e^{-x/\beta}}{\beta^\alpha\Gamma(\alpha)}\); E(X)=αβ; Var=αβ²
Weibull W(α,β)
\(f(x)=\frac{\alpha}{\beta^\alpha}x^{\alpha-1}e^{-(x/\beta)^\alpha}\)
Normal: 68-95-99.7 rule
μ±σ: 68%; μ±2σ: 95%; μ±3σ: 99.7%
Memoryless property
Exponential: P(X>s+t|X>s)=P(X>t)
1
Normal distribution
X~N(50, 25). Find P(45 < X < 60).
SOLUTION
1
\(\sigma=5\). \(Z_1=(45-50)/5=-1\); \(Z_2=(60-50)/5=2\)
2
P(−1<Z<2)=Φ(2)−Φ(−1)=0.9772−0.1587=0.8185
P(45 < X < 60) ≈ 0.8185 ≈ 81.85%

Module 4

Sampling Theory & Confidence Intervals

The Central Limit Theorem is the cornerstone of inferential statistics: sample means are approximately normally distributed regardless of the population distribution, enabling us to construct confidence intervals and conduct hypothesis tests with known error rates.

4.1 Central Limit Theorem & Standard Error

CENTRAL LIMIT THEOREM (CLT)

For a random sample of size n from any population with mean μ and variance σ²: \[\bar{X}\sim N\!\left(\mu,\,\frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)\text{ approximately (for large n)}\] Standard error of the mean: \(\text{SE}=\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)

SAMPLING DISTRIBUTIONS
Z-statistic (σ known)
\(Z=\dfrac{\bar{X}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)
t-statistic (σ unknown)
\(t=\dfrac{\bar{X}-\mu}{s/\sqrt{n}},\;df=n-1\)
Sample proportion SE
\(\text{SE}(\hat{p})=\sqrt{p(1-p)/n}\)
1
CLT problem
Population: μ=100, σ=20. Sample n=64. Find P(X̄ > 105).
SOLUTION
1
SE = σ/√n = 20/8 = 2.5. Z=(105−100)/2.5=2
2
P(X̄>105)=P(Z>2)=1−Φ(2)=1−0.9772=0.0228
P(X̄ > 105) ≈ 0.0228 ≈ 2.28%

4.2 Confidence Intervals

CONFIDENCE INTERVAL FORMULAS
CI for μ (σ known, 95%)
\(\bar{x}\pm1.96\dfrac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\)
CI for μ (σ unknown)
\(\bar{x}\pm t_{\alpha/2,n-1}\dfrac{s}{\sqrt{n}}\)
CI for proportion p
\(\hat{p}\pm z_{\alpha/2}\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n}\)
Common z values
90%: z=1.645; 95%: z=1.96; 99%: z=2.576
Width of CI
Width = 2 × margin of error; wider = less precise
Sample size for margin E
\(n=\left(\dfrac{z_{\alpha/2}\sigma}{E}\right)^2\)
1
Confidence interval for μ
Sample: n=36, x̄=45, s=6. Construct 95% CI for μ.
SOLUTION
1
Since n=36 (large), use z=1.96. SE=s/√n=6/6=1
2
CI: \(45\pm1.96\times1=45\pm1.96=(43.04,\;46.96)\)
95% CI: (43.04, 46.96)
2
t-interval (small sample)
n=10, x̄=72, s=8. Find 95% CI (t₀.₀₂₅,₉ = 2.262).
SOLUTION
1
SE=8/√10=2.53. Margin=2.262×2.53=5.72
2
CI: (72−5.72, 72+5.72)=(66.28, 77.72)
95% CI: (66.28, 77.72)

CLT
\(\bar{X}\sim N(\mu,\sigma^2/n)\)
SE
\(\sigma/\sqrt{n}\)
95% CI (σ known)
\(\bar{x}\pm1.96\cdot\text{SE}\)
Module 5

Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing provides a formal procedure for deciding whether evidence from a sample contradicts a null hypothesis. The Z-test, t-test, χ² test, and F-test each suit different scenarios based on sample size, known parameters, and data type.

5.1 Framework & Z/t Tests

HYPOTHESIS TESTING FRAMEWORK
H₀ and H₁
H₀: null hypothesis (status quo); H₁: alternative (claim to test)
Type I Error (α)
Reject H₀ when true (significance level)
Type II Error (β)
Fail to reject H₀ when false
Z-test (σ known / large n)
\(Z=\dfrac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\)
t-test (σ unknown, small n)
\(t=\dfrac{\bar{x}-\mu_0}{s/\sqrt{n}},\;df=n-1\)
Two-sample t-test
\(t=\dfrac{\bar{x}_1-\bar{x}_2}{s_p\sqrt{1/n_1+1/n_2}}\), \(df=n_1+n_2-2\)
1
One-sample Z-test
Claim: μ=50. Sample: n=100, x̄=52, σ=10. Test at α=0.05 (two-tailed).
SOLUTION
1
H₀: μ=50; H₁: μ≠50. Z=(52−50)/(10/√100)=2/1=2
2
Critical value for two-tailed α=0.05: z_crit=1.96. |Z|=2>1.96
3
Reject H₀. Evidence that μ≠50.
Reject H₀ (Z=2 > 1.96 at α=0.05)

5.2 Chi-square Test & F-test

χ² AND F-TEST
χ² Goodness-of-fit
\(\chi^2=\displaystyle\sum\dfrac{(O_i-E_i)^2}{E_i},\;df=k-1\)
χ² Test of Independence
df=(r−1)(c−1); \(E_{ij}=\frac{\text{Row}_i\times\text{Col}_j}{N}\)
F-test (variance ratio)
\(F=s_1^2/s_2^2\) where \(s_1^2>s_2^2\); df₁=n₁−1, df₂=n₂−1
1
Chi-square goodness-of-fit
Observed: O=[20,30,25,25]; Expected: E=[25,25,25,25] (uniform). Test at α=0.05.
SOLUTION
1
\(\chi^2=\frac{(20-25)^2}{25}+\frac{(30-25)^2}{25}+\frac{(25-25)^2}{25}+\frac{(25-25)^2}{25}=\frac{25+25}{25}=2\)
2
df=k−1=3. χ²_crit(0.05, 3)=7.815. χ²=2 < 7.815
3
Fail to reject H₀. Data fits uniform distribution.
Fail to reject H₀ (χ²=2 < 7.815). Data is uniformly distributed.

χ² test

Type I Error α
Reject true H₀
Module 6

ANOVA & Reliability Theory

Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) tests equality of means across multiple groups simultaneously, avoiding inflation of Type I error from multiple t-tests. Reliability theory quantifies the probability that a system functions over time, essential in engineering and quality management.

6.1 One-way ANOVA (CRD)

ONE-WAY ANOVA TABLE
SS Between (SSA)
\(\text{SSA}=\sum_i n_i(\bar{x}_i-\bar{x})^2\)
SS Within (SSE)
\(\text{SSE}=\sum_{i,j}(x_{ij}-\bar{x}_i)^2\)
F-ratio
\(F=\dfrac{\text{MSA}}{\text{MSE}}=\dfrac{\text{SSA}/(k-1)}{\text{SSE}/(N-k)}\)
Degrees of freedom
Between: k−1; Within: N−k; Total: N−1
Decision rule
Reject H₀ (μ₁=μ₂=…=μₖ) if F > F_crit
Two-way ANOVA (RBD)
Add SS for blocks; df=(r−1)(c−1) for error
1
One-way ANOVA
Three groups (k=3, N=12): SSA=60, SSE=90. Test at α=0.05 (F_crit=4.26).
SOLUTION
1
df₁=k−1=2; df₂=N−k=9. MSA=60/2=30; MSE=90/9=10
2
F=MSA/MSE=30/10=3
3
F=3 < F_crit=4.26 → Fail to reject H₀. No significant difference in means.
F=3 < 4.26. Fail to reject H₀. Means are not significantly different.

6.2 Reliability Theory

RELIABILITY FORMULAS
Reliability function
\(R(t)=P(T>t)=1-F(t)\)
Hazard rate (failure rate)
\(h(t)=\dfrac{f(t)}{R(t)}\)
Exponential model
\(R(t)=e^{-\lambda t};\;h(t)=\lambda\) (constant)
MTTF (exp. model)
\(\text{MTTF}=\int_0^\infty R(t)\,dt=\frac{1}{\lambda}\)
Weibull model
\(R(t)=e^{-(t/\beta)^\alpha}\); MTTF=\(\beta\Gamma(1+1/\alpha)\)
Series system
\(R_s=\prod_i R_i(t)\)
1
Exponential reliability
A component has failure rate λ=0.01/hour. Find R(100h), MTTF, and h(t).
SOLUTION
1
\(R(100)=e^{-0.01\times100}=e^{-1}\approx0.3679\approx36.8\%\)
2
\(\text{MTTF}=1/\lambda=1/0.01=100\) hours
3
Hazard rate h(t)=λ=0.01/hour (constant — exponential distribution)
R(100)≈36.8%; MTTF=100h; h(t)=0.01/h (constant)

Module 7

Multiple Regression & Non-parametric Tests

Multiple regression extends simple regression to multiple predictors, essential for real-world data analysis. Non-parametric tests make no assumption about the population distribution and are used when data is ordinal, skewed, or sample sizes are small.

7.1 Multiple Linear Regression

MULTIPLE REGRESSION
Model equation
\(\hat{y}=b_0+b_1x_1+b_2x_2+\cdots+b_kx_k\)
Matrix form
\(\hat{\mathbf{y}}=X\boldsymbol{\beta}\); \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}=(X^TX)^{-1}X^T\mathbf{y}\)
R² (explained var.)
\(R^2=1-\dfrac{\text{SSE}}{\text{SST}}\in[0,1]\)
Adjusted R²
\(R^2_{adj}=1-\dfrac{\text{SSE}/(n-k-1)}{\text{SST}/(n-1)}\)
Overall F-test
H₀: all βᵢ=0. \(F=\frac{SSR/k}{SSE/(n-k-1)}\)
Individual t-test
\(t=\hat{b}_j/\text{SE}(\hat{b}_j),\;df=n-k-1\)
1
Interpret multiple regression output
Fitted model: ŷ = 5 + 3x₁ + 2x₂, R²=0.85. Interpret β₁.
SOLUTION
1
β₁=3: holding x₂ constant, a unit increase in x₁ increases ŷ by 3 units
2
R²=0.85: 85% of variance in y is explained by x₁ and x₂ — a strong model
β₁=3 (partial effect); R²=85% variance explained

7.2 Non-parametric Tests

NON-PARAMETRIC TEST SUMMARY
Mann-Whitney U test
Two independent samples; ranks combined; H₀: equal distributions. \(U=n_1n_2+\frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2}-R_1\)
Wilcoxon Signed-Rank
Paired samples; rank absolute differences; H₀: symmetric about 0
Kruskal-Wallis
Non-parametric one-way ANOVA. Rank all data; \(H=\frac{12}{N(N+1)}\sum\frac{R_i^2}{n_i}-3(N+1)\sim\chi^2_{k-1}\)
Sign Test
Count + and − signs; binomial distribution with p=0.5
When to use non-parametric
Small samples; non-normal data; ordinal data; outliers present
Advantage
Distribution-free; robust to outliers; works with ranks/ordinal data
1
Mann-Whitney U test
Group A: 3,5,7,9 (n₁=4). Group B: 2,4,6,8 (n₂=4). Ranks combined (ascending): 2(B),3(A),4(B),5(A),6(B),7(A),8(B),9(A). R₁ = sum of ranks for A.
SOLUTION
1
Ranks of Group A: 3→rank2, 5→rank4, 7→rank6, 9→rank8. R₁=2+4+6+8=20
2
\(U=n_1n_2+\frac{n_1(n_1+1)}{2}-R_1=16+10-20=6\)
3
Critical value for n₁=n₂=4, α=0.05: U_crit=1. U=6 > 1 → Fail to reject H₀
U = 6; Fail to reject H₀ (no significant difference between groups)
2
Kruskal-Wallis test
3 groups, N=12. Sum of ranks: R₁=22, R₂=30, R₃=26 (each n=4). Compute H.
SOLUTION
1
\(H=\frac{12}{12(13)}\left[\frac{22^2}{4}+\frac{30^2}{4}+\frac{26^2}{4}\right]-3(13)\)
2
\(=\frac{1}{13}\left[\frac{484+900+676}{4}\right]-39=\frac{1}{13}\cdot\frac{2060}{4}-39=\frac{515}{13}-39=39.62-39=0.62\)
3
df=k−1=2. χ²_crit(0.05,2)=5.991. H=0.62 < 5.991 → Fail to reject H₀
H=0.62 < 5.991. No significant difference among groups.
