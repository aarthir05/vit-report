Module 1

Second Order Linear ODE — CF & PI

The general solution of a 2nd-order linear ODE is GS = CF + PI. The Complementary Function (CF) solves the homogeneous equation via the Auxiliary Equation (AE). The Particular Integral (PI) handles the forcing function using the operator method.

1.1 Complementary Function — Three Cases

CF BASED ON AE ROOTS (m² + am + b = 0)
Real & Distinct roots m₁ ≠ m₂
\(\text{CF}=c_1e^{m_1x}+c_2e^{m_2x}\)
Real & Repeated m₁ = m₂ = m
\(\text{CF}=(c_1+c_2x)e^{mx}\)
Complex roots α ± iβ
\(\text{CF}=e^{\alpha x}(c_1\cos\beta x+c_2\sin\beta x)\)
1
CF — all three cases illustrated
Solve: (a) \(y''-5y'+6y=0\) (b) \(y''-6y'+9y=0\) (c) \(y''-4y'+13y=0\)
SOLUTION
a
AE: \(m^2-5m+6=(m-2)(m-3)=0\Rightarrow m=2,3\). CF: \(c_1e^{2x}+c_2e^{3x}\)
b
AE: \((m-3)^2=0\Rightarrow m=3\)(repeated). CF: \((c_1+c_2x)e^{3x}\)
c
AE: \(m=\frac{4\pm\sqrt{16-52}}{2}=2\pm3i\). CF: \(e^{2x}(c_1\cos3x+c_2\sin3x)\)

1.2 Particular Integral — Operator Method

PI FORMULAS
PI for eᵃˣ (f(a)≠0)
\(\text{PI}=\dfrac{e^{ax}}{f(a)}\)
PI for eᵃˣ (failure: f(a)=0)
Multiply by x; use \(\dfrac{xe^{ax}}{f'(a)}\)
PI for sin(ax)/cos(ax)
Substitute \(D^2=-a^2\); rationalise
PI for xⁿ
Expand \([f(D)]^{-1}\) as binomial series; operate on xⁿ
Shift rule
\(\dfrac{1}{f(D)}e^{ax}V=e^{ax}\dfrac{1}{f(D+a)}V\)
GS
GS = CF + PI (always combine both)
1
Complete solution (CF + PI)
Solve \((D^2-3D+2)y=e^{4x}\).
SOLUTION
1
AE: \((m-1)(m-2)=0\Rightarrow m=1,2\). CF\(=c_1e^x+c_2e^{2x}\)
2
PI: \(\frac{e^{4x}}{f(4)}=\frac{e^{4x}}{16-12+2}=\frac{e^{4x}}{6}\)
y = c₁eˣ + c₂e²ˣ + e⁴ˣ/6
2
PI for sinusoidal — rationalise
Find PI for \((D^2+D+1)y=\sin2x\).
SOLUTION
1
Put \(D^2=-4\): PI\(=\frac{\sin2x}{-4+D+1}=\frac{\sin2x}{D-3}\)
2
Rationalise: \(\frac{(D+3)}{D^2-9}\sin2x=\frac{2\cos2x+3\sin2x}{-4-9}=\frac{-(2\cos2x+3\sin2x)}{13}\)
PI = −(2cos2x + 3sin2x)/13
3
Failure case (f(a) = 0)
Find PI for \((D^2-5D+6)y=e^{2x}\).
SOLUTION
1
f(2)=4−10+6=0 → failure. Use: PI\(=\frac{xe^{2x}}{f'(2)}\). \(f'(D)=2D-5\), \(f'(2)=-1\)
2
PI\(=\frac{xe^{2x}}{-1}=-xe^{2x}\)
PI = −xe²ˣ

Module 2

Variation of Parameters & Euler-Cauchy

Variation of Parameters is a general method for PI when the operator method is insufficient. Euler-Cauchy equations have variable coefficients (xⁿy^(n) terms) and reduce to constant-coefficient equations via the substitution x = eᵗ.

2.1 Variation of Parameters

VARIATION OF PARAMETERS

For \(y''+Py'+Qy=R\) with CF \(c_1y_1+c_2y_2\): \[\text{PI}=y_1v_1+y_2v_2,\quad v_1=-\int\frac{y_2R}{W}dx,\quad v_2=\int\frac{y_1R}{W}dx\] where \(W=y_1y_2'-y_2y_1'\) is the Wronskian.

1
Variation of Parameters
Solve \(y''-2y'+y=\frac{e^x}{x}\) using variation of parameters.
SOLUTION
1
CF: \((m-1)^2=0\Rightarrow y_1=e^x,\;y_2=xe^x\)
2
Wronskian: \(W=e^x(1+x)e^x-xe^x\cdot e^x=e^{2x}\)
3
\(v_1=-\int\frac{xe^x\cdot e^x/x}{e^{2x}}dx=-\int1\,dx=-x\). \(v_2=\int\frac{e^x\cdot e^x/x}{e^{2x}}dx=\int\frac{dx}{x}=\ln x\)
4
PI\(=e^x(-x)+xe^x\ln x=-xe^x+xe^x\ln x\)
y = (c₁+c₂x)eˣ + xeˣ(ln x − 1)
2
VoP with trig forcing
Solve \(y''+y=\sec x\).
SOLUTION
1
CF: \(m=\pm i\Rightarrow y_1=\cos x,\;y_2=\sin x\). \(W=\cos^2x+\sin^2x=1\)
2
\(v_1=-\int\sin x\sec x\,dx=-\int\tan x\,dx=\ln|\cos x|\)
3
\(v_2=\int\cos x\sec x\,dx=x\)
y = c₁cosx + c₂sinx + cosx·ln|cosx| + xsinx

2.2 Euler-Cauchy Equations

EULER-CAUCHY SUBSTITUTION
Substitution
\(x=e^t,\;t=\ln x\)
xy′ = Dy (D=d/dt)
\(xy'=Dy\)
x²y″ = D(D−1)y
\(x^2y''=D(D-1)y\)
1
Euler-Cauchy homogeneous
Solve \(x^2y''-3xy'+4y=0\).
SOLUTION
1
Substitute: \([D(D-1)-3D+4]y=(D^2-4D+4)y=(D-2)^2y=0\)
2
AE: \((m-2)^2=0\Rightarrow m=2\)(repeated). In t: \(y=(c_1+c_2t)e^{2t}\)
3
Back-substitute \(t=\ln x\): \(y=(c_1+c_2\ln x)x^2\)
y = (c₁ + c₂ ln x)x²

Wronskian W
\(y_1y_2'-y_2y_1'\)
v₁ (VoP)
\(-\int y_2R/W\,dx\)
Euler-Cauchy
\(x=e^t\); \(x^2y''=D(D-1)y\)
Module 3

Partial Differential Equations

PDEs govern heat conduction, wave propagation, fluid flow, and quantum mechanics. VIT covers formation, Lagrange's first-order method, and separation of variables for the classical heat and wave equations.

3.1 Formation & Lagrange's Method

LAGRANGE'S METHOD FOR Pp + Qq = R

Auxiliary equations: \(\dfrac{dx}{P}=\dfrac{dy}{Q}=\dfrac{dz}{R}\). Find two independent solutions \(u=c_1\) and \(v=c_2\). General solution: \(\phi(u,v)=0\).

1
Lagrange's method
Solve \(xp+yq=z\).
SOLUTION
1
Auxiliary: \(\frac{dx}{x}=\frac{dy}{y}=\frac{dz}{z}\)
2
From 1st and 2nd: \(\ln x=\ln y+c\Rightarrow x/y=c_1\). From 2nd and 3rd: \(y/z=c_2\)
General solution: φ(x/y, y/z) = 0 i.e., z = y·f(x/y)
2
Form PDE by eliminating constants
Form PDE from \(z=ax^2+by^2\).
SOLUTION
1
\(p=2ax\Rightarrow a=p/(2x)\); \(q=2by\Rightarrow b=q/(2y)\)
2
Substitute: \(z=\frac{p}{2x}x^2+\frac{q}{2y}y^2=\frac{px+qy}{2}\)
PDE: px + qy = 2z

3.2 Separation of Variables

STANDARD PDE FORMS
1D Heat Equation
\(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}=\alpha^2\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}\)
1D Wave Equation
\(\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial t^2}=c^2\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}\)
Laplace Equation
\(\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial x^2}+\dfrac{\partial^2u}{\partial y^2}=0\)
1
Heat equation — separation of variables
Solve \(u_t=u_{xx}\) with \(u(0,t)=u(\pi,t)=0\), \(u(x,0)=\sin x\).
SOLUTION
1
Let \(u=X(x)T(t)\): \(XT'=X''T\Rightarrow\frac{T'}{T}=\frac{X''}{X}=-\lambda^2\)
2
\(X''+\lambda^2X=0\Rightarrow X=A\cos\lambda x+B\sin\lambda x\). BC: X(0)=0→A=0; X(π)=0→λ=n
3
\(T'=-n^2T\Rightarrow T=Ce^{-n^2t}\). IC \(u(x,0)=\sin x\Rightarrow n=1,C=1\)
u(x,t) = sin(x)·e^(−t)

Module 3 — Key Formulas

Lagrange aux.
dx/P = dy/Q = dz/R
Heat Eq.
\(u_t=\alpha^2u_{xx}\)
Sep. variables
u = X(x)·T(t)

Laplace Transforms

The Laplace transform converts ODE problems with initial conditions into algebraic equations in the s-domain, dramatically simplifying their solution. It's the standard tool in control systems, signal processing, and circuit analysis.

4.1 Standard Laplace Transform Table

LAPLACE TRANSFORM TABLE
L{1}
\(1/s\)
L{tⁿ}
\(n!/s^{n+1}\)
L{eᵃᵗ}
\(1/(s-a)\)
L{sin at}
\(a/(s^2+a^2)\)
L{cos at}
\(s/(s^2+a^2)\)
L{eᵃᵗ sin bt}
\(b/((s-a)^2+b^2)\)
L{eᵃᵗ cos bt}
\((s-a)/((s-a)^2+b^2)\)
L{f′(t)}
\(sF(s)-f(0)\)
L{f″(t)}
\(s^2F(s)-sf(0)-f'(0)\)
1
Solve IVP using Laplace Transform
Solve \(y''+4y'+3y=e^{-t}\) with \(y(0)=1,\;y'(0)=0\).
STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
Take LT: \((s^2Y-s+4(sY-1)+3Y=\frac{1}{s+1}\)
2
\(Y(s^2+4s+3)=s+4+\frac{1}{s+1}=\frac{(s+4)(s+1)+1}{s+1}=\frac{s^2+5s+5}{s+1}\)
3
\(Y=\frac{s^2+5s+5}{(s+1)^2(s+3)}\). Partial fractions: \(\frac{A}{s+1}+\frac{B}{(s+1)^2}+\frac{C}{s+3}\)
4
Solving: A=3/4, B=−1/2, C=1/4. Inverse LT: \(y=\frac{3}{4}e^{-t}-\frac{1}{2}te^{-t}+\frac{1}{4}e^{-3t}\)
y = (3/4)e^(−t) − (1/2)te^(−t) + (1/4)e^(−3t)
2
Inverse Laplace by partial fractions
Find \(\mathcal{L}^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{2s+3}{(s+1)(s+2)}\right\}\).
SOLUTION
1
Partial fractions: \(\frac{A}{s+1}+\frac{B}{s+2}\). At s=−1: A=1. At s=−2: B=1.
2
Inverse: \(e^{-t}+e^{-2t}\)
e^(−t) + e^(−2t)

4.2 Shifting Theorems & Convolution

PROPERTIES
First Shifting (s-shift)
\(\mathcal{L}\{e^{at}f(t)\}=F(s-a)\)
Second Shifting (t-shift)
\(\mathcal{L}\{f(t-a)u(t-a)\}=e^{-as}F(s)\)
Convolution Theorem
\(\mathcal{L}\{f*g\}=F(s)G(s)\)

Module 5

Fourier Series, Transforms & Z-Transforms

Fourier series decompose periodic signals into sinusoids. Fourier transforms extend this to non-periodic signals. Z-transforms are the discrete-time counterpart, essential for digital signal processing and difference equations.

5.1 Fourier Series

FOURIER SERIES FORMULAS
General form [−L, L]
\(f(x)=\frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\!\left(a_n\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}+b_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\)
Euler's formulae
\(a_0=\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f\,dx;\;a_n=\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}dx;\;b_n=\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}dx\)
Even function → Cosine series
All bₙ = 0
Odd function → Sine series
All aₙ = 0
Parseval's Theorem
\(\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L[f]^2dx=\frac{a_0^2}{2}+\sum(a_n^2+b_n^2)\)
Famous result
\(\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{1}{n^2}=\frac{\pi^2}{6}\) (from x² expansion)
1
Fourier series of f(x) = x
Find Fourier series of \(f(x)=x\) in \((-\pi,\pi)\).
SOLUTION
1
f(x)=x is odd → a₀=0, aₙ=0. \(b_n=\frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\pi x\sin(nx)\,dx\)
2
IBP: \(b_n=\frac{2}{\pi}\left[-\frac{x\cos nx}{n}+\frac{\sin nx}{n^2}\right]_0^\pi=\frac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\)
\(f(x)=2\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin nx\)

5.2 Z-Transform

Z-TRANSFORM TABLE
Definition
\(Z\{x(n)\}=\sum_{n=0}^\infty x(n)z^{-n}\)
Z{u(n)} unit step
\(z/(z-1)\)
Z{aⁿ}
\(z/(z-a)\)
Z{n·aⁿ}
\(az/(z-a)^2\)
Right shift (delay)
\(Z\{x(n-k)\}=z^{-k}X(z)\)
Initial Value Theorem
\(x(0)=\lim_{z\to\infty}X(z)\)
1
Find Z-transform
Find \(Z\{3^n+2n\}\).
SOLUTION
1
\(Z\{3^n\}=\frac{z}{z-3}\)
2
\(Z\{n\}=Z\{n\cdot1^n\}=\frac{z}{(z-1)^2}\)
3
\(X(z)=\frac{z}{z-3}+\frac{2z}{(z-1)^2}\)
X(z) = z/(z−3) + 2z/(z−1)²
2
Inverse Z-transform
Find \(Z^{-1}\!\left\{\dfrac{z^2}{(z-1)(z-2)}\right\}\).
SOLUTION
1
\(\frac{X(z)}{z}=\frac{z}{(z-1)(z-2)}=\frac{-1}{z-1}+\frac{2}{z-2}\)
2
\(X(z)=\frac{-z}{z-1}+\frac{2z}{z-2}\Rightarrow x(n)=-1+2\cdot2^n=2^{n+1}-1\)
x(n) = 2^(n+1) − 1

Fourier bₙ
\(\frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^L f\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}dx\)
Z{aⁿ}
\(z/(z-a)\)
Z shift
\(Z\{x(n-k)\}=z^{-k}X(z)\)
Module 6

Applications of Differential Equations

Differential equations model virtually every physical system. Spring-mass systems, electrical circuits, population growth, and heat flow all lead to ODEs or PDEs that can be solved using the techniques from previous modules.

6.1 Spring-Mass System

MECHANICAL VIBRATIONS
Newton's 2nd Law
\(m\ddot{x}+c\dot{x}+kx=F(t)\)
Undamped free (c=0,F=0)
\(\ddot{x}+\omega_n^2x=0;\;\omega_n=\sqrt{k/m}\)
Natural frequency
\(f_n=\omega_n/(2\pi)\) Hz
Damping ratio
\(\zeta=c/(2\sqrt{mk})\)
Over/Under/Critical damped
\(\zeta>1\) / \(\zeta<1\) / \(\zeta=1\)
Resonance
Forced frequency = ωₙ; amplitude grows unbounded (undamped)
1
Undamped spring-mass
A 2 kg mass on a spring (k = 50 N/m) is displaced 0.1 m and released. Find x(t).
SOLUTION
1
ODE: \(2\ddot{x}+50x=0\Rightarrow\ddot{x}+25x=0\). \(\omega_n=5\) rad/s
2
General solution: \(x=A\cos5t+B\sin5t\). IC: \(x(0)=0.1\Rightarrow A=0.1\); \(\dot{x}(0)=0\Rightarrow B=0\)
x(t) = 0.1 cos(5t) m

6.2 RLC Circuit

RLC CIRCUIT ODE
Kirchhoff's Voltage Law
\(L\frac{di}{dt}+Ri+\frac{q}{C}=E(t)\); differentiating: \(L\ddot{q}+R\dot{q}+\frac{q}{C}=E(t)\)
Analogies
L↔m, R↔c, 1/C↔k, E(t)↔F(t), q↔x
1
RLC circuit solution
An RLC series circuit has R=4Ω, L=1H, C=0.2F, E=0. Find q(t) if q(0)=1, q'(0)=0.
SOLUTION
1
ODE: \(\ddot{q}+4\dot{q}+5q=0\). AE: \(m^2+4m+5=0\Rightarrow m=-2\pm i\)
2
GS: \(q=e^{-2t}(c_1\cos t+c_2\sin t)\). IC q(0)=1: c₁=1. q'(0)=0: −2c₁+c₂=0 → c₂=2
q(t) = e^(−2t)(cos t + 2 sin t)

6.3 Population Models & Newton's Law of Cooling

FIRST-ORDER APPLICATION MODELS
Exponential growth
\(\frac{dP}{dt}=kP\Rightarrow P=P_0e^{kt}\)
Logistic growth
\(\frac{dP}{dt}=kP\!\left(1-\frac{P}{M}\right)\)
Newton's Law of Cooling
\(\frac{dT}{dt}=k(T-T_\infty)\Rightarrow T=T_\infty+(T_0-T_\infty)e^{kt}\)
1
Newton's law of cooling
A cup of coffee at 90°C cools in a 20°C room. After 5 min, temperature is 70°C. Find k and temperature after 10 min.
SOLUTION
1
\(T=20+(90-20)e^{kt}=20+70e^{kt}\). At t=5: \(70=20+70e^{5k}\Rightarrow e^{5k}=5/7\Rightarrow k=\frac{\ln(5/7)}{5}\approx-0.0673\)
2
At t=10: \(T=20+70e^{10k}=20+70(5/7)^2=20+70(25/49)=20+35.7=55.7°C\)
k ≈ −0.0673 min⁻¹; T(10) ≈ 55.7°C

Module 7

Numerical Methods for ODEs

When ODEs lack closed-form solutions, numerical methods compute accurate approximate solutions step by step. Euler's method is simple; Runge-Kutta 4th order (RK4) is the workhorse of scientific computing, balancing accuracy and efficiency.

7.1 Euler's Method

EULER'S METHOD
Iteration formula
\(y_{n+1}=y_n+h\cdot f(x_n,y_n)\) where \(h\) is step size
Error (local)
O(h²) per step; O(h) globally — first-order method
1
Euler's method
Solve \(y'=x+y\) with \(y(0)=1\). Use h=0.1 for 3 steps.
SOLUTION
1
f(x,y)=x+y. Step 1: \(y_1=1+0.1(0+1)=1.1\)
2
Step 2: \(y_2=1.1+0.1(0.1+1.1)=1.1+0.12=1.22\)
3
Step 3: \(y_3=1.22+0.1(0.2+1.22)=1.22+0.142=1.362\)
4
Exact at x=0.3: \(y=2e^{0.3}-0.3-1=2(1.3499)-1.3=1.3998\approx1.40\). Error≈3%
y(0.1)≈1.1, y(0.2)≈1.22, y(0.3)≈1.362

7.2 Runge-Kutta 4th Order (RK4)

RUNGE-KUTTA 4 FORMULA
Four slopes
\(k_1=hf(x_n,y_n)\)
\(k_2=hf(x_n+h/2,\,y_n+k_1/2)\)
\(k_3=hf(x_n+h/2,\,y_n+k_2/2)\)
\(k_4=hf(x_n+h,\,y_n+k_3)\)
Update
\(y_{n+1}=y_n+\dfrac{1}{6}(k_1+2k_2+2k_3+k_4)\)

Error: O(h⁴) per step; O(h⁵) globally — very accurate
1
RK4 — one step
Apply RK4 to \(y'=x+y,\;y(0)=1\) with h=0.1 to find y(0.1).
STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
\(k_1=0.1\cdot f(0,1)=0.1(0+1)=0.1\)
2
\(k_2=0.1\cdot f(0.05,1.05)=0.1(0.05+1.05)=0.11\)
3
\(k_3=0.1\cdot f(0.05,1.055)=0.1(0.05+1.055)=0.1105\)
4
\(k_4=0.1\cdot f(0.1,1.1105)=0.1(0.1+1.1105)=0.12105\)
5
\(y_1=1+\frac{1}{6}(0.1+0.22+0.221+0.12105)=1+\frac{0.66205}{6}=1+0.11034=1.11034\)
6
Exact: \(2e^{0.1}-1.1=1.11034\). RK4 is exact to 5 decimal places!
y(0.1) ≈ 1.11034 (exact to 5 decimal places)
