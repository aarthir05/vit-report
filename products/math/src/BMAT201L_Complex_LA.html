Module 1

Analytic Functions of Complex Variables

A function f(z) = u + iv is analytic if it satisfies the Cauchy-Riemann equations — a pair of coupled PDEs ensuring complex differentiability. Analytic functions model potential flow, electrostatics, and are the foundation of conformal mapping.

1.1 Cauchy-Riemann Equations

CAUCHY-RIEMANN (CR) EQUATIONS
CR in Cartesian
\(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}=\dfrac{\partial v}{\partial y},\quad\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial y}=-\dfrac{\partial v}{\partial x}\)
CR in Polar (z=re^{iθ})
\(u_r=\dfrac{1}{r}v_\theta,\quad\dfrac{1}{r}u_\theta=-v_r\)
Derivative
\(f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=v_y-iu_y\)
Harmonic function
\(\nabla^2u=u_{xx}+u_{yy}=0\)
Harmonic conjugate
Given u, find v using CR so that f=u+iv is analytic
Entire function
Analytic everywhere (e.g., eᶻ, sin z, polynomials)
1
Verify analyticity — eᶻ
Show \(f(z)=e^z\) is entire and find \(f'(z)\).
SOLUTION
1
\(e^z=e^x(\cos y+i\sin y)\Rightarrow u=e^x\cos y,\;v=e^x\sin y\)
2
\(u_x=e^x\cos y=v_y\) ✓ and \(u_y=-e^x\sin y=-v_x\) ✓ CR satisfied everywhere
3
\(f'(z)=u_x+iv_x=e^x\cos y+ie^x\sin y=e^z\)
f(z) = eᶻ is entire; f′(z) = eᶻ
2
Find harmonic conjugate
Given \(u=x^3-3xy^2\), find harmonic conjugate v and f(z).
SOLUTION
1
Check: \(u_{xx}=6x,\;u_{yy}=-6x\Rightarrow\nabla^2u=0\) ✓
2
By CR: \(v_y=u_x=3x^2-3y^2\Rightarrow v=3x^2y-y^3+g(x)\)
3
\(v_x=6xy+g'(x)=-u_y=6xy\Rightarrow g'(x)=0\Rightarrow g=c\)
4
\(f(z)=(x^3-3xy^2)+i(3x^2y-y^3)=z^3+ic\)
v = 3x²y − y³ + c; f(z) = z³

1.2 Milne-Thomson Method

MILNE-THOMSON METHOD

To construct f(z) from u(x,y) without finding v explicitly: \[f(z)=\int\left[u_x(z,0)-iu_y(z,0)\right]dz+C\] (Replace x→z, y→0 in the partial derivatives.)

1
Milne-Thomson method
Find f(z) if \(u=e^x(x\cos y-y\sin y)\).
SOLUTION
1
\(u_x=e^x[(x+1)\cos y-y\sin y]\). At y=0: \(u_x(z,0)=e^z(z+1)\)
2
\(u_y=e^x(-x\sin y-\sin y-y\cos y)\). At y=0: \(u_y(z,0)=0\)
3
\(f(z)=\int e^z(z+1)dz=\int ze^z dz+\int e^z dz=e^z(z-1)+e^z+C=ze^z+C\)
f(z) = zeᶻ + C

Module 2

Complex Integration — Cauchy's Theory

Cauchy's integral theorem and formula are among the most powerful results in mathematics — they evaluate complex integrals purely from function values at isolated points. Taylor and Laurent series classify function behaviour near singularities.

2.1 Cauchy's Theorem & Integral Formula

CAUCHY'S INTEGRAL THEOREM

If f(z) is analytic on and inside a simple closed contour C: \(\oint_C f(z)\,dz=0\)

CAUCHY'S INTEGRAL FORMULA

If f(z) analytic inside C and a inside C: \[f(a)=\frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{z-a}\,dz\qquad f^{(n)}(a)=\frac{n!}{2\pi i}\oint_C\frac{f(z)}{(z-a)^{n+1}}\,dz\]

1
Apply CIF
Evaluate \(\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=3}\dfrac{e^z}{z-2}\,dz\).
SOLUTION
1
f(z)=eᶻ analytic everywhere; a=2 inside |z|=3 ✓
2
By CIF: \(=2\pi i\cdot f(2)=2\pi ie^2\)
2πie²
2
Higher-order CIF
Find \(\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=2}\dfrac{\cos\pi z}{(z-1)^3}\,dz\).
SOLUTION
1
f(z)=cos πz, a=1 (inside |z|=2), n=2. Need f″(1).
2
\(f'(z)=-\pi\sin\pi z,\;f''(z)=-\pi^2\cos\pi z\). \(f''(1)=-\pi^2\cos\pi=-\pi^2(-1)=\pi^2\)
3
\(\oint=\frac{2\pi i}{2!}f''(1)=\pi i\cdot\pi^2=\pi^3i\)
π³i

2.2 Taylor & Laurent Series

SERIES & SINGULARITY TYPES
Taylor (analytic region)
\(f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(z-z_0)^n\)
Laurent (annular region)
\(f(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(z-z_0)^n+\sum_{n=1}^\infty\frac{b_n}{(z-z_0)^n}\)
Removable singularity
All bₙ=0; limit exists and is finite
Pole of order m
bₘ≠0, bₙ=0 for n>m; finite principal part
Essential singularity
Infinitely many bₙ≠0
Simple pole (m=1)
Only b₁≠0
1
Laurent expansion
Expand \(f(z)=\dfrac{1}{z(z-1)}\) for \(0<|z|<1\).
SOLUTION
1
Partial fractions: \(\frac{-1}{z}+\frac{1}{z-1}\)
2
For |z|<1: \(\frac{1}{z-1}=\frac{-1}{1-z}=-\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n\)
3
\(f(z)=\frac{-1}{z}-\sum_{n=0}^\infty z^n=\frac{-1}{z}-1-z-z^2-\cdots\)
f(z) = −1/z − 1 − z − z² − ··· (simple pole at z=0)

Module 3

Residues & Applications to Real Integrals

The Residue Theorem converts complex contour integrals into a simple sum of residues at poles. This powerful technique solves otherwise impossible real definite integrals, evaluating integrals of rational, trigonometric, and improper functions.

3.1 Residue Theorem

RESIDUE FORMULAS
Residue Theorem
\(\oint_C f(z)\,dz=2\pi i\displaystyle\sum_k\text{Res}(f,z_k)\)
Residue at simple pole z=a
\(\text{Res}(f,a)=\lim_{z\to a}(z-a)f(z)\)
Residue at pole order m
\(\text{Res}(f,a)=\dfrac{1}{(m-1)!}\lim_{z\to a}\dfrac{d^{m-1}}{dz^{m-1}}[(z-a)^mf(z)]\)
1
Evaluate by residue theorem
Evaluate \(\displaystyle\oint_{|z|=3}\dfrac{z^2}{(z+1)^2(z-2)}\,dz\).
SOLUTION
1
Poles: z=−1 (order 2, inside) and z=2 (simple, inside)
2
Res at z=2: \(\lim_{z\to2}(z-2)\cdot\frac{z^2}{(z+1)^2(z-2)}=\frac{4}{9}\)
3
Res at z=−1 (order 2): \(\frac{d}{dz}\left[\frac{z^2}{z-2}\right]_{z=-1}=\frac{z^2-4z}{(z-2)^2}\Big|_{z=-1}=\frac{1+4}{9}=\frac{5}{9}\)
4
Total: \(2\pi i\left(\frac{4}{9}+\frac{5}{9}\right)=2\pi i\)
2πi
2
Real integral by contour integration
Evaluate \(\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\dfrac{d\theta}{5-3\cos\theta}\).
SOLUTION
1
Let z=e^{iθ}, dθ=dz/(iz), cosθ=(z+z⁻¹)/2
2
Integral becomes \(\oint_{|z|=1}\frac{2\,dz}{i(3z^2-10z+3)}=\oint\frac{2\,dz}{3i(z-3)(z-1/3)}\)
3
Only z=1/3 inside |z|=1. Res at 1/3: \(\frac{2}{3i(1/3-3)}=\frac{2}{3i(-8/3)}=\frac{1}{4i}\)
4
\(I=2\pi i\cdot\frac{1}{4i}=\frac{\pi}{2}\)
π/2

Module 4

Conformal Mapping

Conformal mappings preserve angles locally and transform complex domains into simpler shapes where boundary value problems become tractable. The bilinear (Möbius) transformation is the most important class, mapping circles and lines to circles and lines.

4.1 Bilinear (Möbius) Transformation

BILINEAR TRANSFORMATION
General form
\(w=\dfrac{az+b}{cz+d},\quad ad-bc\neq0\)
Fixed points
Solve \(cz^2+(d-a)z-b=0\)
Cross-ratio (invariant)
\(\dfrac{(w-w_1)(w_2-w_3)}{(w-w_3)(w_2-w_1)}=\dfrac{(z-z_1)(z_2-z_3)}{(z-z_3)(z_2-z_1)}\)
1
Find bilinear transformation from 3 points
Find bilinear map sending \(z_1=1,z_2=i,z_3=-1\) to \(w_1=0,w_2=1,w_3=\infty\).
SOLUTION
1
Since w₃=∞, the map is \(w=\frac{z-z_1}{z-z_3}\cdot\frac{z_2-z_3}{z_2-z_1}\)
2
\(w=\frac{z-1}{z+1}\cdot\frac{i+1}{i-1}\). Now \(\frac{i+1}{i-1}=\frac{(i+1)(-i-1)}{2}=-i\)
3
\(w=\frac{-i(z-1)}{z+1}\)
w = −i(z−1)/(z+1)
2
Fixed points
Find fixed points of \(w=\dfrac{6z-9}{z}\).
SOLUTION
1
Set w=z: \(z^2=6z-9\Rightarrow(z-3)^2=0\Rightarrow z=3\) (repeated — parabolic type)
Fixed point z = 3 (repeated)

4.2 Standard Conformal Mappings

STANDARD MAPPINGS
w = z² (squaring)
Upper half z-plane → full w-plane; doubles angles at origin
w = eᶻ
Strip 0<y<π → upper half w-plane
w = z + 1/z (Joukowski)
Circles → airfoil profiles; aerodynamics applications

Module 5

Vector Spaces & Linear Transformations

Vector spaces provide the abstract framework for linear algebra. Matrices, polynomials, and functions are all vector spaces. Linear transformations preserve the space's structure, with rank and nullity measuring their fundamental properties.

5.1 Vector Spaces, Subspaces & Basis

KEY DEFINITIONS
Subspace test (3 conditions)
Non-empty; closed under addition; closed under scalar multiplication
Linear independence
\(c_1v_1+\cdots+c_nv_n=0\Rightarrow\) all cᵢ=0
Basis
Linearly independent + spans V
Dimension
Number of vectors in any basis of V
Rank-Nullity Theorem
\(\text{rank}(T)+\text{nullity}(T)=\dim(\text{domain})\)
Basis test via det
det≠0 ↔ linearly independent (square matrix)
1
Check if set forms a basis
Is \(\{(1,0,1),(0,1,0),(1,1,0)\}\) a basis for ℝ³?
SOLUTION
1
\(\det\begin{pmatrix}1&0&1\\0&1&0\\1&1&0\end{pmatrix}=1(0-0)-0+1(0-1)=-1\neq0\)
2
det ≠ 0 → linearly independent + n=3 vectors for ℝ³ → it is a basis ✓
Yes, forms a basis for ℝ³
2
Find rank and nullity
Find rank and nullity of \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1&3\\2&4&1&5\\1&2&2&4\end{pmatrix}\).
SOLUTION
1
R2←R2−2R1; R3←R3−R1: \(\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1&3\\0&0&3&-1\\0&0&3&1\end{pmatrix}\)
2
R3←R3−R2: \(\begin{pmatrix}1&2&-1&3\\0&0&3&-1\\0&0&0&2\end{pmatrix}\). Three pivots → Rank = 3
3
Nullity = 4 − 3 = 1 (by Rank-Nullity Theorem)
Rank = 3, Nullity = 1

5.2 Linear Transformations

LINEAR TRANSFORMATIONS
Definition
T(u+v)=T(u)+T(v) and T(cu)=cT(u)
Kernel (null space)
\(\ker(T)=\{v:T(v)=\mathbf{0}\}\)
Image (range)
\(\text{Im}(T)=\{T(v):v\in V\}\)
1
Matrix of linear transformation
T(x,y)=(x+2y, 3x−y). Find matrix [T] and check invertibility.
SOLUTION
1
T(e₁)=T(1,0)=(1,3); T(e₂)=T(0,1)=(2,−1). Matrix: \([T]=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&-1\end{pmatrix}\)
2
det=−1−6=−7≠0 → invertible ✓
Matrix [[1,2],[3,−1]]; invertible (det=−7)

Module 6

Inner Products, Eigenvalues & Cayley-Hamilton

Inner product spaces generalise geometry to abstract spaces, enabling orthogonality and projection. Eigenvalues reveal the most fundamental behaviour of transformations; Cayley-Hamilton is a powerful algebraic identity with many computational applications.

6.1 Gram-Schmidt Orthogonalisation

GRAM-SCHMIDT PROCESS
Projection
\(\text{proj}_u v=\dfrac{\langle v,u\rangle}{\|u\|^2}u\)
Step 1
\(e_1=v_1\)
Step k
\(e_k=v_k-\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\frac{\langle v_k,e_j\rangle}{\|e_j\|^2}e_j\)
1
Gram-Schmidt
Apply Gram-Schmidt to \(v_1=(1,1,0),\;v_2=(1,0,1)\).
SOLUTION
1
\(e_1=(1,1,0)\); \(\|e_1\|^2=2\)
2
\(\langle v_2,e_1\rangle=1+0+0=1\). \(e_2=v_2-\frac{1}{2}e_1=(1,0,1)-(1/2,1/2,0)=(1/2,-1/2,1)\)
3
Check orthogonality: \(\langle e_1,e_2\rangle=1/2-1/2+0=0\) ✓
e₁=(1,1,0); e₂=(1/2,−1/2,1) (orthogonal pair)

6.2 Eigenvalues & Cayley-Hamilton Theorem

EIGENVALUE THEORY
Characteristic equation
\(\det(A-\lambda I)=0\)
Eigenvector
Solve \((A-\lambda I)\mathbf{v}=\mathbf{0}\) for each λ
tr(A) = Σλ
Sum of eigenvalues = trace
det(A) = Πλ
Product of eigenvalues = determinant
Diagonalisation
\(A=PDP^{-1}\) where P = eigenvectors, D = diag(λ)
Cayley-Hamilton
Every matrix satisfies its characteristic equation: φ(A)=O
1
Eigenvalues and eigenvectors
Find eigenvalues and eigenvectors of \(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\).
SOLUTION
1
Char. eq.: \(\det\begin{pmatrix}3-\lambda&1\\1&3-\lambda\end{pmatrix}=(3-\lambda)^2-1=0\Rightarrow\lambda^2-6\lambda+8=0\Rightarrow(\lambda-2)(\lambda-4)=0\)
2
λ₁=2: \((A-2I)v=0\Rightarrow\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\1&1\end{pmatrix}v=0\Rightarrow v_1=(1,-1)^T\)
3
λ₂=4: \((A-4I)v=0\Rightarrow\begin{pmatrix}-1&1\\1&-1\end{pmatrix}v=0\Rightarrow v_2=(1,1)^T\)
λ=2: v=(1,−1); λ=4: v=(1,1)
2
Cayley-Hamilton — find A⁻¹
Verify Cayley-Hamilton for \(A=\begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}\) and find A⁻¹.
SOLUTION
1
Char. eq.: λ²−5λ−2=0. By C-H: \(A^2-5A-2I=O\)
2
\(A^2=\begin{pmatrix}7&10\\15&22\end{pmatrix}\). Check: \(\begin{pmatrix}7&10\\15&22\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}5&10\\15&20\end{pmatrix}-\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}\) ✓
3
From C-H × A⁻¹: \(A-5I-2A^{-1}=0\Rightarrow A^{-1}=\frac{A-5I}{2}=\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}-4&2\\3&-1\end{pmatrix}\)
A⁻¹ = (1/2)[[−4,2],[3,−1]]

Module 7

Quadratic Forms & Matrix Decompositions

Quadratic forms classify conic sections and surfaces. Positive definiteness determines stability of equilibria. LU decomposition solves linear systems efficiently. Orthogonal diagonalisation (spectral theorem) is the foundation of principal component analysis (PCA).

7.1 Quadratic Forms & Positive Definiteness

QUADRATIC FORMS
Quadratic form Q
\(Q(\mathbf{x})=\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}\) where A is symmetric
Positive definite (PD)
All eigenvalues >0; or all leading principal minors >0
Positive semi-definite
All eigenvalues ≥0
Negative definite
All eigenvalues <0
Indefinite
Mixed signs of eigenvalues
Sylvester's criterion (PD)
All leading principal minors Δ₁, Δ₂, … >0
1
Classify a quadratic form
Determine if \(Q=2x^2+4xy+3y^2\) is positive definite.
SOLUTION
1
Matrix: \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&2\\2&3\end{pmatrix}\) (off-diagonal = coefficient/2)
2
Leading minor Δ₁=2>0. Δ₂=det(A)=6−4=2>0.
3
Both minors positive → Q is positive definite
Q is positive definite (Sylvester's criterion satisfied)

7.2 LU Decomposition

LU DECOMPOSITION
Goal
Factor A = LU where L = lower triangular (1s on diagonal), U = upper triangular. Then solve Ly=b (forward sub) and Ux=y (back sub)
Efficiency
Once A=LU is computed, multiple RHS vectors b can be solved with just O(n²) operations vs O(n³) for Gaussian elimination each time
1
LU Decomposition
Find LU decomposition of \(A=\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\1&3&1\\-1&2&6\end{pmatrix}\).
STEP-BY-STEP SOLUTION
1
R2←R2−(1/2)R1; R3←R3+(1/2)R1. Multipliers: \(\ell_{21}=1/2,\;\ell_{31}=-1/2\)
2
After step 1: \(\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\0&1&2\\0&4&5\end{pmatrix}\)
3
R3←R3−4R2. Multiplier \(\ell_{32}=4\). U=\(\begin{pmatrix}2&4&-2\\0&1&2\\0&0&-3\end{pmatrix}\)
4
\(L=\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\1/2&1&0\\-1/2&4&1\end{pmatrix}\)
L = [[1,0,0],[1/2,1,0],[−1/2,4,1]]; U = [[2,4,−2],[0,1,2],[0,0,−3]]

7.3 Spectral Theorem & Orthogonal Diagonalisation

SPECTRAL THEOREM (SYMMETRIC MATRICES)

Every real symmetric matrix A has:

  • All real eigenvalues
  • Eigenvectors from different eigenvalues are orthogonal
  • A = QDQᵀ where Q is orthogonal (Qᵀ = Q⁻¹) and D = diagonal eigenvalue matrix
1
Orthogonal diagonalisation
Orthogonally diagonalise \(A=\begin{pmatrix}3&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}\).
SOLUTION
1
From Module 6: λ₁=2 with v₁=(1,−1)ᵀ; λ₂=4 with v₂=(1,1)ᵀ. Orthogonal ✓ (symmetric A)
2
Normalise: \(\hat{v}_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,-1)^T\), \(\hat{v}_2=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)^T\)
3
\(Q=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\\-1&1\end{pmatrix}\), \(D=\begin{pmatrix}2&0\\0&4\end{pmatrix}\). Then A=QDQᵀ
A = QDQᵀ with Q orthogonal, D = diag(2, 4)
