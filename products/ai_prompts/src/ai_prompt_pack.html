📧 Category 1: Writing Professional Emails
Stop staring at a blank email. These prompts get you a polished draft in seconds — just fill in the details.
01
Follow-up after no reply
Use when: A client, colleague, or vendor hasn't responded in 3–7 days
Write a polite follow-up email to [recipient] regarding [topic]. I sent an email on [date] and haven't received a response. Keep it brief, friendly, and non-pushy. End with a clear next step.
02
Declining a request professionally
Use when: You need to say no without damaging the relationship
Write a professional email declining [request/invitation] from [recipient]. Reason for declining: [reason]. Keep it respectful, offer an alternative if possible, and maintain the relationship.
03
Requesting an extension or deadline change
Use when: You need more time on a deliverable
Write an email to [recipient/manager] requesting an extension for [task/project]. Original deadline: [date]. Proposed new deadline: [date]. Reason: [brief reason]. Keep it accountable — don't make excuses, just be clear and solution-focused.
04
Escalation email
Use when: An issue isn't being resolved and needs senior attention
Write a professional escalation email to [senior person/manager] about [issue]. Background: [brief context]. Steps already taken: [what you tried]. I need [specific resolution]. Keep the tone factual, not emotional.
05
Apologising for a mistake
Use when: You need to own an error professionally
Write a professional apology email to [recipient] for [mistake/delay/oversight]. Acknowledge the error clearly, briefly explain what happened, state what you're doing to fix it, and reassure them it won't recur. Tone: sincere, not defensive.
06
Cold introduction email
Use when: Reaching out to someone you've never spoken to
Write a concise cold introduction email to [recipient] at [company]. My name is [name] and I work as [role] at [company]. Purpose: [what I want — collaboration, advice, a meeting]. Keep it under 5 sentences. Be specific about why I'm contacting them.
07
Thanking someone after help
Use when: Someone went out of their way for you
Write a warm but professional thank-you email to [name] for [specific help they gave]. Mention how it helped me: [specific outcome]. Keep it genuine and not over-the-top. 3–4 sentences max.
08
Summarising a long email thread
Use when: An email chain has gotten confusing and needs a recap
Summarise this email thread and write a clean summary email I can send to all parties. Extract: the key decisions made, open action items, who is responsible for each, and next steps. Here is the thread: [paste thread]
09
Rewriting an email to sound more professional
Use when: Your draft sounds too casual or unclear
Rewrite this email to sound more professional and clear. Improve grammar, tone, and structure. Keep the core message the same. Here is my draft: [paste draft]
10
Announcing a change to a team
Use when: Communicating a policy, process, or personnel change
Write an internal announcement email for the team about [change/update]. What's changing: [describe]. When it takes effect: [date]. Why it's happening: [reason]. Any action required from the team: [actions]. Tone: clear, positive, reassuring.

📄 Category 2: Summarising Documents and Content
Turn long documents, reports, and articles into clear, usable summaries in seconds.
11
Summarise a long document
Use when: You have a long report, policy, or article to digest quickly
Summarise this document in [5 bullet points / 2 paragraphs / 1 page]. Focus on: the main purpose, key findings or decisions, and any action items. Audience: [who will read this]. Here is the document: [paste content]
12
Executive summary
Use when: Creating a one-page summary for senior leadership
Write an executive summary of the following document for senior leadership. Include: situation overview, key findings, recommendations, and next steps. Max 250 words. Here is the content: [paste content]
13
Extract key action items
Use when: Reading meeting notes, emails, or reports for to-dos
Read the following content and extract only the action items. For each one, list: what needs to be done, who is responsible (if mentioned), and the deadline (if mentioned). Format as a table. Here is the content: [paste]
14
Compare two documents
Use when: You need to find differences between two versions or proposals
Compare these two documents and identify the key differences. Focus on: changes in scope, tone, numbers, and recommendations. Present the comparison as a table with Document A vs Document B. Document A: [paste]. Document B: [paste]
15
Summarise for a non-technical audience
Use when: You need to explain technical content to leadership or clients
Summarise this technical document for a non-technical business audience. Avoid jargon. Use plain language. Explain what it means in practice and why it matters. Max [length]. Here is the document: [paste]
16
Create a TL;DR
Use when: You want a 2–3 sentence bottom-line summary
Write a TL;DR (too long; didn't read) summary of this content in exactly 2–3 sentences. Capture the single most important point someone needs to know. Here is the content: [paste]
17
Extract risks and concerns
Use when: Reviewing a proposal, contract, or report for potential problems
Read the following document and list all risks, concerns, or potential problems mentioned or implied. Rate each as High / Medium / Low risk. Suggest a mitigation for each. Here is the document: [paste]
18
Summarise a meeting recording transcript
Use when: You have a raw Teams/Zoom transcript
Here is a meeting transcript. Summarise it with: 1) A 3-sentence overview of what was discussed 2) Key decisions made 3) Action items with owner names 4) Any unresolved questions. Here is the transcript: [paste]
19
Bullet-point a dense paragraph
Use when: You need to make dense content readable
Convert this dense paragraph into clear, scannable bullet points. Each bullet should be one idea. Use plain language. Here is the paragraph: [paste]
20
Identify the main argument
Use when: Reading a long proposal or research paper
Read this document and identify: 1) The central argument or recommendation 2) The top 3 pieces of evidence supporting it 3) Any counterarguments mentioned 4) The conclusion. Here is the document: [paste]

📊 Category 3: Writing Reports and Documents
Build structured, professional reports faster — from status updates to full project reports.
21
Weekly status report
Write a professional weekly status report for [project/role]. This week I completed: [list]. In progress: [list]. Blockers: [list or "none"]. Next week's priorities: [list]. Audience: [manager/team]. Keep it concise and structured.
22
Project proposal
Write a professional project proposal for [project name]. Include: problem statement, proposed solution, scope, timeline, resources needed, expected benefits, and risks. Audience: [stakeholders]. Format as a formal document.
23
Post-mortem / lessons learned report
Write a post-mortem report for [project/incident]. Include: what went well, what went wrong, root causes, impact, and recommendations to prevent recurrence. Context: [paste notes]. Tone: factual, blame-free, improvement-focused.
24
Business case for a new tool or investment
Write a business case to justify purchasing [tool/service/resource]. Include: current problem, proposed solution, cost estimate, expected ROI or benefits, risks of not acting, and a recommendation. Audience: finance/senior leadership.
25
Incident report
Write a professional incident report for [incident type] that occurred on [date]. Include: timeline of events, immediate impact, response actions taken, root cause, and corrective actions. Keep it factual and chronological.
26
Competitive analysis
Help me write a competitive analysis comparing [our product/service] against [competitor 1] and [competitor 2]. Compare on: pricing, features, strengths, weaknesses, and target audience. Present as a table followed by a paragraph summary.
27
Convert bullet notes into a formal report section
Convert these rough bullet point notes into a polished, professional report section. Add transitions, fix grammar, and make it formal. Section heading: [heading]. My notes: [paste notes]
28
SWOT analysis
Create a detailed SWOT analysis for [company/project/team/strategy]. For each of the four areas — Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats — provide 4–5 specific, realistic points based on this context: [paste context or describe situation]
29
Monthly management report intro
Write a professional introduction paragraph for a monthly management report for [month]. Key highlights this month: [list 3–4 points]. Overall performance: [positive/mixed/challenging]. Tone: confident, factual, forward-looking.
30
Policy document draft
Draft a professional policy document for [policy topic] for [company/department]. Include: purpose, scope, policy statement, responsibilities, procedures, and compliance requirements. Tone: formal and unambiguous.

📅 Category 4: Meeting Preparation and Follow-up
Be the most prepared person in every meeting — without spending hours getting ready.
31
Create a meeting agenda
Create a structured meeting agenda for a [duration] meeting about [topic]. Attendees: [roles]. Goal of the meeting: [goal]. Include: welcome/intro, agenda items with time slots, discussion points for each, and a closing/next steps section.
32
Generate questions to ask in a meeting
I have a [type of meeting] about [topic] with [who]. Generate [10] smart, relevant questions I should ask. Context about the meeting: [paste any relevant background]. Include both clarifying questions and strategic ones.
33
Write meeting minutes
Convert these meeting notes into formal meeting minutes. Include: meeting date, attendees, agenda items discussed, key decisions, action items with owners and deadlines, and next meeting date. Notes: [paste notes]
34
Prepare for a difficult conversation
Help me prepare for a difficult conversation with [role/relationship] about [issue]. Give me: how to open the conversation, key points to cover, how to respond if they get defensive, and how to end constructively. My goal: [what outcome I want].
35
Post-meeting follow-up email
Write a professional follow-up email after our meeting about [topic] on [date]. Summarise: what was decided, action items with owners, and next steps. Recipients: [names/roles]. Tone: clear and action-oriented.
36
Talking points for a presentation to leadership
Give me 5 clear talking points for presenting [topic] to senior leadership. Each talking point should have: a headline sentence, 2 supporting facts or details, and a so-what (why it matters). Background: [paste context]
37
Brainstorm meeting — generate ideas
Generate [15] creative but practical ideas for [problem/challenge]. For each idea: state it in one sentence and give a one-line explanation of how it works. Context: [describe the problem]. Don't filter — include bold and conservative ideas both.
38
One-on-one preparation
Help me prepare for a one-on-one meeting with my [manager/direct report]. Topics I want to cover: [list]. Help me frame each topic clearly, anticipate their likely response, and suggest what I should ask for or offer in each case.
39
Summarise a presentation into key messages
Read this presentation content and extract the 3–5 key messages the audience should remember. For each: state the message in one sentence and explain why it matters. Content: [paste slides text or notes]
40
Handle objections in a meeting
I'm proposing [idea/change/project] in a meeting. Likely objections I'll face: [list 3 objections]. For each objection, give me a confident, data-driven response I can use. Keep responses concise — 2–3 sentences each.
🧠 Category 5: Explaining Technical Concepts Simply
Bridge the gap between technical teams and business stakeholders.
41
Explain like I'm 10
Explain [technical concept] as if you're explaining it to a 10-year-old. Use an everyday analogy. Avoid all technical jargon. Keep it under 5 sentences.
42
Explain to a non-technical manager
Explain [technical concept/system/finding] to a business manager who has no technical background. Focus on: what it is, why it matters to the business, and what action (if any) is needed. No acronyms without explanation.
43
Create an FAQ for a technical change
Write an FAQ document about [technical change/system update] for non-technical staff. Include [8–10] questions they're likely to ask, with clear plain-language answers. Context: [describe the change]
44
Translate a technical error into plain language
Explain what this technical error or message means in plain English. What caused it? What should the user do next? Keep it simple and actionable. Error: [paste error message or technical finding]
45
Write a glossary of terms
Create a plain-language glossary for these [number] technical terms used in [project/domain]. Each definition should be 1–2 sentences, jargon-free, with a real-world example where possible. Terms: [list terms]
46
Explain the "so what" of data
Here is some data or analysis: [paste data/findings]. Explain what this means in business terms. What is the key insight? What decision or action does this support? Write 3–4 sentences a senior leader would find useful.
47
Analogy for a complex process
Create a simple, memorable analogy that explains [complex process or concept] to someone with no background in [field]. The analogy should make the core idea immediately obvious.
48
Write a one-pager explanation
Write a one-page plain-language explainer about [topic] for [audience]. Include: what it is, why it matters, how it works (in simple terms), and what it means for [audience specifically]. No more than 400 words.
49
Explain pros and cons simply
Explain the pros and cons of [technology/approach/decision] for a non-technical decision maker. 4–5 pros and 4–5 cons. Each point: one sentence, no jargon, business-focused impact.
50
Write a change communication message
Write an all-staff communication about [technical change]. Explain: what is changing, why, when it happens, how it affects them day-to-day, and who to contact with questions. Tone: clear, calm, reassuring. No technical jargon.
